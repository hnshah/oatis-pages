## INTELLIGENCE BRIEF: Silicon Valley Bank
Analysis Date: March 21, 2026
Overall Risk Level: 9.0/10 — CRITICAL

---

Executive Summary

Silicon Valley Bank entered receivership on March 10, 2023, following disclosure of an after-tax loss two days prior. The signal sequence shows a compressed crisis timeline: routine filings through January 2023 gave no public warning before the loss disclosure on March 8, followed immediately by the receivership filing on March 10. This represents a complete institutional failure with minimal advance notice in regulatory disclosures.

---

Signal Timeline

September–January 2022–2023 (Pre-Crisis Phase)
SVB filed routine 8-K disclosures over a five-month window with no escalating severity indicators. These included general corporate disclosures (September 12, December 6), executive compensation actions (September 14), routine financial events (October 20, January 19), and a corporate governance matter (November 23). None of these filings triggered elevated severity ratings. This period reflects normal operational activity with no visible distress signals in regulatory documents.

March 8, 2023 (Loss Disclosure)
SVB filed an 8-K disclosing an after-tax loss, rated 8/10 severity. This represents a material shift from the prior six months of routine filings. The loss disclosure appeared in Items 7.01 (Regulation FD Disclosure) and 9.01 (Financial Statements), indicating the bank made a proactive disclosure of adverse financial results rather than being forced by external action.

March 10, 2023 (Receivership)
Two days after the loss disclosure, SVB filed an 8-K announcing bankruptcy/receivership with 9/10 severity (Items 1.03 and 8.01). This represents the terminal event—the filing that formalized the institution's collapse.

Critical Timing: The interval between the first public signal of material distress (March 8) and institutional failure (March 10) was 48 hours. Prior regulatory filings provided zero advance warning.

Layer Analysis

Regulatory Layer (Elevated)

All eight signals are regulatory filings. The layer elevation reflects the concentration of distress signals in formal SEC disclosures rather than warning signs in financial performance, market activity, or operational changes.

The two high-severity signals (8/10 and 9/10) compress into a 48-hour window in early March 2023. The loss disclosure on March 8 is the first explicit acknowledgment of financial distress visible in the signal record. The receivership filing on March 10 is the institutional endpoint—it formalizes what the loss disclosure implied.

The six low-severity signals (2/10) spanning September 2022 through January 2023 are genuinely routine. They include compensation appointments, earnings events, and governance matters. None contain language, structure, or SEC item codes that suggest financial distress. If deterioration was occurring, it was not disclosed incrementally—it appeared abruptly.

---

Cross-Layer Patterns

There are no elevated layers outside the regulatory domain in this dataset. The absence of signals from financial, operational, or market layers during the September 2022–January 2023 period is itself notable. Either those layers did not generate detectable signals, or the data collection did not capture them.

The concentration of all signals in regulatory filings means we are observing forced disclosures rather than voluntary market intelligence. The pattern suggests SVB's distress materialized rapidly enough that regulatory filings—the most formal, lagging indicators—became the primary evidence trail.

The 48-hour collapse window following the loss disclosure indicates a cascading loss of confidence. The loss disclosure itself likely triggered deposit flight, regulatory action, or both, which compressed the runway from public disclosure to receivership into two business days.

What to Watch Next

1. Deposit Flight Velocity (March 8–10, 2023 — Completed)
Monitor historical data on deposit outflows during the 48-hour window between the loss disclosure and receivership. This will confirm whether the loss disclosure triggered immediate large withdrawals that forced FDIC intervention. High-velocity outflows (>$10B per day) would suggest confidence collapse rather than gradual deterioration.

2. Interest Rate Sensitivity in SVB's Asset Base (Pre-March 2023)
Examine SVB's disclosed portfolio composition and duration in late 2022 filings. The loss disclosed on March 8 likely reflects unrealized losses on fixed-income securities as the Fed raised rates through 2022. Quarterly reports filed in late 2022 and early 2023 would show whether the bank had disclosed growing duration/rate risk in regulatory language. Absence of such disclosure would indicate risk was either not flagged internally or deliberately obscured.

3. Regulatory Examination Timeline (FDIC/Fed Records)
Request or analyze when federal bank examiners last rated SVB as healthy versus when concerns were raised. If the most recent public or examination data (prior to March 8) showed satisfactory capital ratios, this indicates a rapid deterioration or a gap between regulatory surveillance and actual risk. If examiners had flagged concerns earlier, the question becomes why those were not visible in March 2022–February 2023 filings.

4. Peer Bank Filings for Latent Rate Risk (March 2023 Comparison)
Cross-reference SVB's loss disclosure with contemporaneous 10-Q filings from other mid-sized regional banks (PacWest, Western Alliance, Comerica) to determine whether the rate-driven loss was idiosyncratic to SVB or a sector-wide phenomenon. If peers disclosed similar unrealized losses, SVB's failure becomes a visibility/confidence issue rather than unique mismanagement.

5. Insolvency Determination vs. Liquidity Crisis (Post-Receivership Analysis)
Clarify whether SVB failed due to actual insolvency (liabilities exceeded assets) or liquidity crisis (unable to access deposits). The two-day window and rapid receivership suggest liquidity cascade; the loss disclosure suggests underlying insolvency. Early FDIC reports will specify this distinction and indicate whether the loss disclosed on March 8 was already known to regulators or came as a surprise.

Confidence Assessment

Confidence Level: High (85%)

What the data shows with certainty:
- SVB filed routine disclosures with no distress indicators through January 2023
- A material loss was disclosed on March 8, 2023
- Receivership followed on March 10, 2023
- The total public warning window was 48 hours

What confidence gaps remain:

The dataset lacks operational, market, and deposit-level signals. We are analyzing only formal SEC filings. SVB's distress may have been visible in real-time market signals (CDS spreads, deposit flow data, borrower surveys) that are not captured here. The apparent "sudden" failure in regulatory filings may reflect a lag in disclosure rather than actual surprise to markets or regulators.

The loss disclosed on March 8 could have been known to examiners weeks or months earlier without being reflected in 8-K filings. Conversely, it could represent a genuine surprise. Without access to examination reports, call report data (FFIEC filings), or Fed correspondence, we cannot determine whether regulators were aware of deterioration before March 8.

Signals most likely to be false positives:
The six 2/10 severity signals from September 2022–January 2023 are genuinely low-risk operational filings. They are not false positives; they are true negatives—confirmations that no distress was publicly disclosed during that period. However, they do not rule out concurrent private deterioration.

Signal that would change the assessment:
Discovery of earlier regulatory warnings, deposit outflow data, or internal bank communications predating March 8 would demonstrate that the 48-hour collapse window was a confidence cascade, not a surprise failure. This would lower the "sudden surprise" component of the risk but would not change the severity of the ultimate outcome.
